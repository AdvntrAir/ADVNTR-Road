trailheadSummary: >
  This was a weather week, and specifically a heat-and-water week. The through-line running from the North Cascades down to Zion and out to the Gunnison is the same physics wearing four costumes: a hot, dry Western summer is turning ordinary July into a season of active hazard. Lightning from a late-June storm is still throwing new fires in the North Cascades a full three weeks later, with three burning at once. Yellowstone did something anglers rarely see mid-season — closed its three marquee rivers to fishing outright because the water got hot enough to kill trout. Zion shuttered the Narrows and every slot canyon ahead of monsoon cells. Black Canyon of the Gunnison had a road buried under six feet of debris with people still at the bottom of it. None of these are freak events on their own; the pattern is that they are all happening in the same seven days, and that the parks are managing them with a workforce thinner than it was two summers ago. If you are traveling the interior West this month, the honest planning posture is to assume something on your itinerary will be closed when you arrive, and to build the trip so that is an inconvenience rather than a wasted drive.

The practical lesson is scheduling: the Narrows is a lottery-permit hike for a reason, and building a rigid single-day plan around it in mid-to-late July is a gamble against the weather. Monsoon cells can trigger flooding from rain falling miles away under clear local skies, so the closure often looks arbitrary from the trailhead but rarely is.
