This corridor rewards active management more than passive cruising. Anything larger than a standard van needs real awareness of narrow shoulders, steep cliffside turns on the southern half, and genuinely competitive parking at every popular stop. Transmission control and actual adherence to posted speed advisories matter here in a way they don't on most West Coast drives — this isn't a highway you set cruise control on and forget. The upside: the anchor campgrounds all handle 40-footers without drama, so the challenge is in transit, not in camp.

Tent campers should look hard at Tillicum Beach — direct ocean-edge sites under pine canopy, close enough to fall asleep to the surf. The tradeoff is real: no showers, no dump station, dry-camping infrastructure only. Pair a Tillicum stay with a state park stop nearby if you need to actually shower mid-trip.

Day-trippers have it easiest of anyone. The corridor is built for picking up high-value overlooks, tide-pool walks, and seafood stops directly off the highway without camping at all — a single well-timed day covering Cape Perpetua, a tide-pool window, and a resupply town hits the highlights without the logistics of an overnight.

Three experiences here don't exist in this form anywhere else on the West Coast. The first is legal beach driving: a specific 10-mile stretch of hard-packed sand starting at the Columbia River mouth near Warrenton permits street-legal vehicles directly on the beach. It requires dropping tire pressure for the dune entry ramps and a 12-volt compressor to reinflate before returning to pavement — not a casual detour, but worth planning around if it's on your list. The second is dune riding out of Florence: rental outfitters run ATVs and dune buggies onto the Oregon Dunes National Recreation Area's forty miles of open sand, the largest coastal dune system in North America. It takes its own paperwork — a day pass or Forest Service pass to park, plus a separate state ATV Operating Permit for the vehicle — but it's the one activity on this corridor that has nothing to do with tide charts. The third is traditional crabbing on the southern bays. The docks at Charleston and Bandon are where it happens; pack windproof layers and rugged gloves, and see the Field Notes section for the license anyone 12 and up needs first.
