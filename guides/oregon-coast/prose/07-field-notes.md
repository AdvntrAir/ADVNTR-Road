The highway itself is free — no toll, no gate, no entrance station anywhere in 363 miles. What changed going into 2026 is everything around it. Oregon State Parks restructured its fee system over the past year, and several figures commonly cited in older trip reports are now wrong. Day-use parking is no longer free at Fort Stevens or Bullards Beach: both began charging $10 a day in-state ($12 out-of-state) in October 2025, and the 12-month parking permit doubled to $60 for residents ($75 non-resident) in January 2026. Fort Stevens added a $10-per-use fee to its previously free dump station in March 2026. An America the Beautiful pass covers the federal sites — Tillicum Beach, the Cape Perpetua day-use area — but is not accepted at Oregon State Parks. Nearly all fee machines are cashless.

Camping costs stack the same way. State park sites run $35 to $52 a night with rates topping out on peak summer dates, Tillicum Beach runs $39 to $51 depending on hookups, and the private resorts start around $60 and climb past $120. A newly approved fee structure could push Oregon's highest-demand state parks as high as $80 a night during peak periods — none of it prohibitive, but a traveler budgeting off a two-year-old blog post will be surprised more than once.

The one license that trips people up: anyone 12 or older needs an Oregon Shellfish License before crabbing or clamming — $13 a year for residents in 2026, with day licenses and non-resident rates running higher. It's purchased instantly online at myodfw.com or at bait shops in any gateway town. There's no backcountry permit system to navigate here; the corridor's paperwork is parking and shellfish, full stop.

The central coast carries its own small paperwork stack, all separate from the state park system. Yaquina Head Outstanding Natural Area charges $7 per vehicle, good for three days, with an America the Beautiful pass accepted. The Oregon Dunes National Recreation Area requires a $5 Forest Service digital day pass (or a Northwest Forest Pass or America the Beautiful annual) to park at most staging areas — and if you're riding an ATV or dune buggy rather than just parking, that's a separate Oregon ATV Operating Permit on top of the parking pass, not instead of it. Sea Lion Caves, unlike everything else on this list, is a private attraction: roughly $18 for an adult ticket, and no public land pass of any kind applies.

Cell coverage is reliable in Astoria, Newport, and Coos Bay and genuinely absent on the open cliffside stretches south of Yachats and inside the forested campground loops. Downloaded maps, offline NOAA tide charts, and a trail app with offline tiles are the working minimum. Emergencies are 911 everywhere on the corridor.

Seasonally, the corridor doesn't close — it erodes. US-101 stays open year-round outside genuine storm emergencies, but winter surf undermines the cliffside shoulders every year, and summer is when the state fixes what winter broke. Expect flag crews and single-lane pacing on a July or August drive; it's not a red flag, it's the corridor's normal rhythm. Peak season is July through September; the shoulder months trade fast-moving weather for empty campgrounds; and late November through March is a dramatic, storm-battered experience for travelers equipped for it — not for casual first-timers.
