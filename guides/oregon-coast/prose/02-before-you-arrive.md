Resupply strategy on this corridor is simple in theory and easy to get wrong in practice: stock up big at the anchor towns, because everything in between is small, seasonal, and priced like it knows it's your only option. Astoria is the northern staging ground — full grocery, fuel, propane, hardware, and mechanical support, all before the road gets more remote heading south. It's where you do your homework before the coast asks you to improvise.

Newport, roughly the corridor's true midpoint, does double duty: full-service grocery and fuel alongside a genuine commercial fishing fleet and a hospital, if you need one. Florence, another sixty miles south, stages the dune-riding half of the trip — fuel, groceries, and the ATV and dune buggy rental lots that line the highway at the edge of Old Town.

Coos Bay does the same resupply job further south, with a rougher edge and better tools: full-service supermarkets, warehouse bulk stores, commercial diesel, and marine mechanics who can fix what the drive down broke. Charleston, just west of town, is the actual draw if you want to crab or fish rather than just restock.

Port Orford breaks the pattern in a different way: almost no big-box infrastructure at all, but worth the stop regardless. This is a quiet, historic fishing village that most 101 traffic drives straight through without a second look, and that's a mistake — treat it as a lunch stop, not a staging stop.

One constraint applies at every gateway on this corridor: parking a large rig downtown is a real problem, not a minor inconvenience. These historic cores were built before RVs existed, and it shows. Scout your parking before committing to a viewpoint or a lunch stop — Astoria's historic district in particular is not a place to arrive with a 40-footer and no plan.
