The North Coast is where most people's mental image of this drive comes from, and it earns that reputation honestly — the Astoria Column, Haystack Rock, a shipwreck you can walk right up to. It's also the most crowded, most infrastructure-heavy stretch of the whole corridor, and treating it that way saves you a lot of frustration. If you're piloting anything bigger than a van, build your day around beating the crowds to the parking lot rather than fighting them for a spot at noon.

> Anything larger than a camper van should be parked at the marquee stops before 9:00 AM. A midday backing maneuver in a full trailhead lot is how vacation days end early.

Cannon Beach is the one stop on this entire corridor that isn't optional. Haystack Rock rises 235 feet at the center of a two-mile crescent of open sand between Ecola Creek and Tolovana Park, and the tidepools at its base are one of only seven protected Marine Gardens on the Oregon coast — arrive an hour before low tide and the intertidal shelf is genuinely thick with starfish and anemones, not the picked-over version you get at busier stops. The nearest public lot sits at Hemlock and Gower by City Hall, and it fills fast on summer weekends; this is a town built for foot traffic, not rigs, so plan to walk in from wherever you can find pavement. A few minutes north, Ecola State Park adds the view most photos of this stretch are actually taken from — a headland overlook above the whole bay, Haystack Rock included, for a ten-dollar day-use fee. Budget half a day here at minimum; people who plan for an hour end up staying for four.

What earns this zone its place isn't just the famous stops — it's how fast the terrain changes underneath them. One mile you're threading a paved lot at a beach shipwreck with a hundred other cars; the next you're alone under moss-draped old-growth canopy on the walk down to Short Sand Beach, where the water is cold enough that a wetsuit isn't optional gear, it's a requirement. Tillamook, a few miles inland, does double duty as both a culinary landmark and a working dairy town — expect a real, unglamorous farm smell hanging over the valley alongside the ice cream lines. That's not a flaw. It's what makes the Creamery feel like an actual place instead of a rest stop with a gift shop.

Budget more time here than the mileage suggests. The density of stops, plus the crowds, means the North Coast eats hours faster than the map implies. When the famous stops feel oversaturated, the Neskowin suspension bridge trail is the quiet alternative — two and a half miles of old growth that most of the boardwalk crowd never finds.
