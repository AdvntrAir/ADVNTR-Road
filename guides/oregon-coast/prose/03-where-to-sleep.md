The state park network is the corridor's safe default, and for anyone driving something long and heavy it's barely a decision: level paved pads, full hookups, hot showers, and interior roads wide enough that backing in isn't the day's main event. Fort Stevens anchors the north end and Bullards Beach the south, with Tillicum Beach — federal, oceanfront, and gloriously shower-free — splitting the difference on the central coast.

There is a second answer for the right rig. Fifteen minutes inland from the highway, free primitive Bureau of Land Management and National Forest land runs through the coastal mountain range — no fees, no crowds, and no reservation system to fight. If you're driving something that needs a level, serviced pad, stay on the coast. If you're driving something that can handle a forest road and you'd rather skip the fees entirely, turn east.

Two practical notes for the public parks. First, most coastal sites don't have individual water spigots at every pad — fill stations are centralized and seasonal, so arrive with fresh water if you can. Second, root heave is common on the older asphalt pads; carry leveling blocks or SnapPads and expect to use them. And everywhere on this corridor, the reservation math is the same: six-month booking windows, and summer weekends gone months before the weather forecast exists.
