Newport and Florence are where the Oregon Coast's postcard version gives way to its working, resupplying, engine-driven middle — the stretch the north's crowds and the south's cliffs both overshadow, and the one first-time visitors are most likely to just drive through. That's a mistake. This sixty-mile segment holds the tallest lighthouse tower in the state, the largest expanse of coastal sand dunes in North America, and a sea cave that has hosted a wild Steller sea lion colony for longer than anyone's kept written records.

Yaquina Head Outstanding Natural Area, just north of Newport, is worth the seven-dollar vehicle fee for the tidepools alone — sprawling basalt shelves thick with anemones and sea stars at low tide, with a 93-foot lighthouse standing over the headland since 1873. The fee is good for three days, and an America the Beautiful pass covers it. A few miles south, Newport's historic Bayfront still runs on a working fishing fleet rather than just tourist shops, and the Oregon Coast Aquarium sits right on Yaquina Bay if the weather turns.

> Sea lions at Sea Lion Caves are wild animals, not a zoo exhibit. Staff will tell you honestly if the colony isn't in the cave that day, with live monitors in the lobby to check first — and a discount plus a return pass if it isn't.

South toward Florence, the highway trades cliffs for the Oregon Dunes National Recreation Area — forty miles of shifting sand and the largest coastal dune system in North America, the one stretch of this corridor built around engines instead of quiet. Rental outfitters in Florence run ATVs and dune buggies onto the open sand; riders need a Northwest Forest Pass or the $5 Forest Service day pass just to park, plus a separate Oregon ATV Operating Permit for the vehicle itself. Jessie M. Honeyman Memorial State Park, a few miles south, is the reason most people camp here at all — two warm freshwater lakes for swimming, direct dune access from certain loops in the off-season, and one of the most in-demand campgrounds on the entire coast.
