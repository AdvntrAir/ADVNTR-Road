Mount Rainier is a 14,411-foot active stratovolcano dominating the Cascade skyline of western Washington. The park divides practically into two main visitor corridors - Paradise on the southwest and Sunrise on the northeast - plus a southeastern old-growth zone anchored by Ohanapecosh, and a northwest sector (Carbon River / Mowich Lake) that is fully closed to vehicle traffic in 2026 due to the permanent failure of the Fairfax Bridge on SR-165.

**Big Rig Reality:**
The historical conflict between sources regarding interior park driving is a simple issue of rig stratification. The park's tight infrastructure makes interior navigation entirely dependent on length. Large setups (35+ feet) must base camp outside the park boundaries (Packwood) and enter uncoupled. Mid-sized setups (24-30 feet) can handle Cougar Rock but face severe tank limitations due to the localized utility closures.

Rainier doesn't require introduction. You can see it from Seattle on a clear day, a white mass above the horizon that makes everything else in the skyline look provisional. Up close, the scale becomes something else - a volcano that hasn't slept in several hundred years, wrapped in 26 glaciers, its flanks broken into distinct worlds that don't much resemble each other.

Most visitors arrive on the southwest side, through the Nisqually Entrance and up WA-706 to Paradise, the park's most-visited corridor and the one with the best infrastructure for first-timers. The northeast side - Sunrise, at 6,400 feet - is higher, emptier, and in the opinion of anyone who's been to both, more viscerally connected to the actual mountain. The southeast corner along Ohanapecosh sits in old-growth forest thick enough to make the alpine zones feel like another planet.

For 2026, the park's northwest quadrant - Carbon River, Mowich Lake, Tolmie Peak - is entirely off-limits to vehicle traffic after the permanent closure of the Fairfax Bridge on SR-165. If you had those trails on a list, move them to 2031 at the earliest. Furthermore, the iconic old-growth trees of the Grove of the Patriarchs are completely inaccessible until bridge replacement construction begins in 2027, and the entire Ohanapecosh campground network is dark for a full utility rebuild.

One thing changed significantly this year: the park dropped its timed-entry reservation pilot entirely. No corridor passes required. That's good news for spontaneity and bad news for parking. Expect Paradise and Sunrise lots to fill by eight in the morning on any sunny weekend. Get there early, or get there late.
