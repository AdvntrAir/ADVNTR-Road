Most people who say they've "done" North Cascades have driven a highway, not entered a park. That's not a knock — it's the honest shape of the place. The actual North Cascades National Park is roadless wilderness, glaciated and vertical, reached on foot or not at all. What you'll drive is State Route 20, threading through Ross Lake National Recreation Area, which wraps the park on both sides like a frame around a painting you can only see the edges of.

There's no entrance gate, no fee, no ranger checking a pass at a booth. You simply arrive, and the highway does the work of showing you glacial lakes, sub-alpine passes, and more ice than anywhere else in the lower 48 — over 300 glaciers, more than the rest of the Cascades combined. What the road doesn't do is prepare you for how completely you drop off the grid. Cell service ends somewhere past Marblemount and doesn't return until Mazama. Plan as if you're leaving civilization, because for the length of that drive, you are.

2026 has been a rough year for this corridor. A December storm and a March rockslide near Diablo Lake shut a 26-mile stretch of SR-20 for nearly six months — far past the routine winter closure this road normally sees. It reopened June 14, and the fixes look durable, but the park has also layered on a campfire ban across the entire complex starting June 29. None of this should scare you off. It should make you check current conditions before you leave rather than trust last year's blog post.

> This is a park built for travelers who've already done Rainier and Olympic and want the version with nobody else on the trail.
