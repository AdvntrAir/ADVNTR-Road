**Fees**

The standard vehicle entrance fee is $30 for seven days during peak season (May 22 through October 31) and $20 in the off-season window (November 1 through May 21). Motorcycles run $25 in summer and $15 in winter. Walk-in or bicycle entry is $15 per person. The park's annual pass is $55 and covers Crater Lake plus Lava Beds National Monument, Lassen Volcanic, and Whiskeytown — worthwhile if you're visiting twice in a year. America the Beautiful passes are accepted and are the better buy for multi-park trips; the 2026 full-price pass is $80 and covers all federal recreation sites.

Free entrance days in 2026 follow the standard NPS calendar — check nps.gov for specific dates before assuming one lines up with your trip.

One rule with no exceptions: the park is cashless. Entrance stations take debit, credit, or pre-purchased digital passes only. Cash is not accepted anywhere in the entrance queue. If your trip depends on paying at the gate, confirm your card is functional beforehand, or buy your pass online in advance and arrive with a confirmation number.

**Construction note for 2026**

The East Rim Drive is under active paving and rehabilitation in 2026. Expect up to 20-minute delays on segments around Crater Peak, Sun Notch, and Vidae Falls. The delays are intermittent rather than constant, but they're real, and a plan that depends on completing the full rim loop in three hours needs buffer for this.

**Permits**

Backcountry overnight camping requires a permit, which is free and self-issued at the Steel Information Center ranger station on the day of your departure. Walk-up only; no advance reservations. Bear canisters are encouraged but not required — a properly rigged counter-balance hang is permitted in designated forest zones. No canister rentals are available on-site.

**Cell service**

Coverage is spotty across most of the park and nonexistent in the places you most want to be. The rim parking areas near Rim Village and the immediate vicinity of Crater Lake Lodge pick up a weak signal occasionally. The canyon basins, the Pinnacles Road corridor, and the remote East Rim segments do not. Download your maps before you arrive — Gaia GPS, AllTrails Pro, or the official NPS app's offline profile for this park. This isn't optional; it's the navigation plan once you're inside the boundary.

**Cleetwood Cove Trail — the closure in plain terms**

The Cleetwood Cove Trail and marina are closed for structural rehabilitation through the 2028 season. Construction begins summer 2026 and the targeted reopening is summer 2029. During the closure: no public trail access to the lake, no swimming, no boat tours. The trailhead parking lot at the north end of the Rim Drive is blocked and under active construction. There is nothing to see at that location that would make it worth driving out to look.

This is a multi-year project on a trail that had been showing serious structural deterioration for a decade. When it reopens, the rebuilt trail and marina will be safer and more accessible than the original. Until then, plan accordingly.

**Seasonal reality**

Peak season — July through September — is the only window when the entire park is genuinely accessible and fully operational. All roads, all trails, the campground, the Lodge, the concessionaire food operations. It's also when the park is busiest and when the rim overlooks hold traffic by midday. September is consistently the best month of the three: the summer crowds thin, the temperatures are comfortable for hiking, and the wildfire smoke risk that plagues August has typically cleared.

Shoulder season (May–June and October) is genuinely volatile. A good day in late June looks like full summer; a bad one looks like winter. Rim Drive segments stay snow-blocked into June some years. October can deliver early blizzards or crisp, clear fall days with nobody else around. If you're visiting shoulder season, check conditions obsessively starting a week before your trip.

Off-season (November through April) converts this park into a completely different experience — quiet, snow-choked, limited to cross-country skiers and snowshoers for most of the loop, with only the road to Rim Village plowed for standard vehicles. Snow chains or approved traction tires are required to ascend. It's a legitimate winter destination for travelers equipped for it and a poor destination for anyone who isn't.
