**RV and van travelers**

Park at Mazama Campground and treat the rim as a day trip rather than something you drive your full rig through. The East Rim Drive is the specific problem — narrow lanes, low rock overhangs, and enough afternoon traffic to turn a tight section into a standstill for a rig over 35 feet. The 2026 construction work adds an additional layer of unpredictability to those segments. Leave the tow vehicle at Mazama and drive something smaller, or plan rim stops for early morning when traffic is thinner and pullout space is actually available.

The Pinnacles Road spur is the one exception: it's built for you. Fully paved, terminates in a genuine large-vehicle roundabout, and gives you access to one of the park's most distinctive walks without the anxiety of a narrow rim overlook.

**Tent campers**

Mazama Campground puts you inside the park with no morning gate delay, which matters more than it sounds when your goal is a Garfield Peak summit before the Rim Village parking fills. Early trailhead access is a genuine advantage. What you sign up for is the cold: nighttime temperatures at 6,000 feet in the valley basin drop below freezing into July. A three-season sleeping bag is insufficient. Bring the right kit or be miserable by 3 AM.

**Hotel and lodge travelers**

The Crater Lake Lodge is the only hotel in the park and one of the more specific lodging experiences in the American West — a 1915 stone building on the rim, rocking chairs on a porch over the caldera, no air conditioning because it's unnecessary at 7,000 feet. The lake-view rooms book out a year in advance. Standard rooms have more availability but still fill in summer. The Cabins at Mazama Village at $200 a night serve travelers who want a real bed without the Lodge price point, with the caveat that you're giving up the rim view entirely.

If the Lodge and the Cabins are both booked: Prospect's Historic Hotel, 28 miles west, is a genuinely good alternative basecamp with better food than anything inside the boundary.

**Day-trippers**

Arrive at the South Entrance before 7:30 AM — the mid-morning backup at Annie Springs is real and can run 30 minutes or more in peak summer. Drive the West Rim Drive clockwise, stop at Discovery Point for an easy short walk that delivers full lake exposure, and get your photographs before the midday sun flattens the blue. The Watchman Peak Trail is the right choice if you want one hike — sunset timing makes it worthwhile but requires protecting your parking spot by arriving at the overlook before 4 PM on clear evenings.

What's different from pre-2026 planning: there's no reason to head for the north rim early to catch a boat tour or beat the Cleetwood line. The trail is closed. Spend that energy on the West Rim Drive itself, where the views are denser and the parking is easier than the north end.

**The two experiences worth protecting time for, regardless of traveler type**

*Watchman Peak at sunset.* The hike is short enough (1.6 miles, 420 feet) that it fits into any itinerary. The lookout at the top frames Wizard Island and the western caldera wall in a composition that's specific to this spot and this time of day — the shadow of the western rim stretches across the lake like a blue wedge as the light drops. This is the visual moment that defines the park in the Cleetwood-closed era.

*The Pinnacles.* Most visitors don't make the 35-minute drive to the Pinnacles Overlook because it requires leaving the rim loop, and the rim loop is where all the visual payoff seems to live. The payoff at the Pinnacles is different rather than lesser: a canyon full of hollow volcanic spires that tell a completely different story about what happened here 7,700 years ago. The walk is twenty minutes and requires nothing. It's the best use of a quiet morning in the park.
