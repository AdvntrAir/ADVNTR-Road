Sleeping inside the park boundary means one of three options: a campsite at Mazama, a cabin at Mazama Village, or a room at Crater Lake Lodge on the rim. Each is a genuinely different trip.

**Mazama Campground** is the operational choice — 214 sites across multiple loops in an old-growth lodgepole forest seven miles south of the rim, with the full range from dry tent sites through electric-only to complete full-hookup pull-throughs that handle rigs to 50 feet. Loop F has coin showers, which are worth the quarters after a dusty day on the rim. The campground opens June 12 and runs through October 4 in 2026, with reservations required through the federal booking system — and those reservations go fast. The window opens six months in advance, and peak-season sites fill within hours of going live, not days. Set a calendar reminder and be ready at your computer when the window opens, or plan around FCFS camping at the shoulder seasons instead.

One thing about Mazama that matters for tent campers specifically: the campground sits in a valley basin at 6,000 feet, and nighttime temperatures here drop below freezing with regularity well into July. The rim's daytime warmth tells you nothing about what the forest floor will feel like at 3 AM. Bring a genuinely cold-rated sleeping bag, not a three-season kit borrowed from a lower-elevation trip.

**Crater Lake Lodge** is on the rim itself — built in 1915, still operating, 71 rooms ranging from standard lodge rooms to lake-view suites at rates between $302 and $409 or more per night. There's no air conditioning, which is not a problem at 7,000 feet; there are oscillating fans and a dining room that serves three meals a day. The experience that justifies the price tag isn't the room, it's the porch: a row of rocking chairs overlooking the caldera, available to guests and day visitors alike, where the light on the water changes every hour. Book a year in advance for lake-view rooms specifically — they go almost instantly. Standard rooms open up closer to the date but fill fast in summer.

**The Cabins at Mazama Village** occupy the middle ground: 40 rooms in rustic A-frame fourplex buildings, $200 a night, within walking distance of the campground amenities. Cooking is prohibited in the rooms, so meals mean the Annie Creek Restaurant next door or a drive up to the Lodge dining room. They're a solid alternative to camping if you want a real bed without paying Lodge prices, and they run from May 8 through October 4. Book through the same concessionaire as the Lodge, also well in advance.

**Lost Creek Campground**, the park's other tent-only option in the southeast corner, has been closed for multiple consecutive seasons. The 2026 closure status is consistent with the 2024 and 2025 closures per NPS records, though it's worth confirming directly at the conditions portal before you build a plan around it. If you're specifically interested in the Pinnacles Road area basecamp experience, that's the right check to make.

**Outside the boundary**

If Mazama is full, the Rogue River-Siskiyou National Forest just west of the park's western gate offers dispersed camping that some experienced travelers prefer to the campground — quiet, free, and genuinely remote, but requiring a fully self-contained rig and solid road clearance for the gravel forest roads. It's a deliberate choice for people built for it, not a fallback for anyone expecting hookups.

**Diamond Lake RV Park**, 25 miles north near the North Entrance, is the best full-hookup alternative for big rigs when Mazama is booked — 110 sites with 30/50-amp service, showers, laundry, no vehicle length restriction. About 40 minutes from the rim. Not a perfect substitute for being inside the park, but substantially more comfortable than a dispersed site and a clear second choice for larger rigs.
