Three towns handle the approach to Crater Lake, and they divide labor clearly enough that knowing which one does what will save you a return trip down the mountain.

**Klamath Falls** is the resupply anchor, 45 miles south of the Annie Springs entrance. Full grocery stores, propane, standard-priced fuel, laundromats, a medical center — everything you'd want to stock up on before driving into a place where the only in-park store charges mountain markups on everything. Crater Lake Gateway RV Park and Klamath Falls KOA Journey are both here if you want to stage overnight and let your body adjust to the base elevation before adding another 3,000 feet. Waffle Hut and Rodeo's Pizza & Salad Kitchen are reliable stops for a meal before the climb.

**Chiloquin** is 33 miles out and smaller — fuel, basic groceries, a post office, and Crater Lake Junction Carvers for a last hot meal with some local character. Don't count on it for a serious resupply; it's a top-off stop, not a grocery run.

**Prospect**, 28 miles west of the South Entrance, is the quiet alternative most people overlook. Small general store and lodging, access to Upper Rogue River waterfalls if you want a detour, and the Prospect Historic Hotel Dinner House — a restored stagecoach dining room that punches well above its weight and makes a legitimate case for basing out of Prospect rather than fighting for space at Mazama Village. Worth knowing about before you commit to a plan.

**Getting in**

The South Entrance via Highway 62 is the year-round default — steady two-lane climb, passes directly by the Steel Information Center headquarters, and connects to the Annie Springs gate without drama. It also carries the bulk of peak-season traffic, which means real backups during midday hours in July and August. Arriving by 8 AM is a materially different experience from arriving at 11.

The North Entrance is a flatter, more direct approach from Highway 138, but it stays snow-blocked longer than the South each year and closes first in fall. Check current conditions before you build a route around it, especially outside the July–September window.

**Two things to sort before you leave**

The entrance stations are cashless — credit or debit card or a digital pass, full stop, since 2023. Confirm yours is working and consider buying your pass online in advance to skip the transaction at the gate entirely. An America the Beautiful Pass is the better buy if you're visiting more than one federal site this year.

The second thing: in 2026, the East Rim Drive is undergoing paving and rehabilitation work. Expect up to 20-minute construction delays on segments around Crater Peak, Sun Notch, and Vidae Falls. This matters for planning your loop timing — if you're intending to drive the East Rim in the afternoon, add meaningful buffer, not a polite five minutes.

**Fuel**

There is one gas station inside the park, at Mazama Village, with predictable mountain pricing. Fill up completely in Klamath Falls or Medford before you start the climb. Running the tank down to "I'll fill at the park" is a plan that works out fine financially but costs more than it should.

**What to carry for cell service**

Coverage is spotty at best across most of the park and essentially nonexistent anywhere you actually want to be — the canyon basins, the Pinnacles Road spur, the remote East Rim segments. Download Gaia GPS or AllTrails Pro maps offline and the NPS app's Crater Lake profile before you lose signal. This is not a nice-to-have; it's the actual plan for navigating once you're inside the boundary.
