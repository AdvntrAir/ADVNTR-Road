Most visitors never make it out here, and the zone rewards that neglect: the crowds thin almost immediately once you turn off the Rim Drive onto Pinnacles Road.

The Volcanic Outskirts & Pinnacles covers the park's quieter southeastern margin — a different geological story from the caldera above. The Pinnacles Trail, Plaikni Falls, and Mount Scott each offer something the rim doesn't, and none of them require the same planning intensity as a peak-season rim stop.

**The Pinnacles Trail** is the zone's simplest stop and among the most visually distinctive things in the park. The six-mile Pinnacles Road spur runs fully paved to a large looping roundabout — built specifically to accommodate big rigs, with no turnaround anxiety — and from the overlook, the trail runs less than a mile along the rim of Wheeler Creek canyon looking out at a field of jagged fossil fumaroles: hollow rock chimneys, colored grey and brown, formed when hot volcanic ash cooled and steam escaped through narrow vents. The formations are 30 to 60 feet tall. The walk is flat, takes twenty minutes, and requires no hiking conditioning whatsoever. Bring a lens cloth — the fine pumice dust in this zone gets into camera gear and clothes. This is also one of the park's construction-zone-free routes for 2026, which makes the drive itself easier than the East Rim alternative.

**Plaikni Falls Trail** is the park's best kept secret for the simple reason that it involves a waterfall, which the park doesn't have anywhere else. The trailhead sits off the same Pinnacles Road, 1.2 miles from the Rim Drive turnoff — a small paved lot that fills by midday, so arrive in the morning if possible. The trail itself is 2.0 miles round trip, gains 100 feet, and runs through shaded old-growth hemlock and fir to the base of a 20-foot falls fed by snowmelt percolating through volcanic soil. Late morning is the best light. The trail is largely accessible to most fitness levels, and the forest keeps it cool even on hot summer days. Note: no trailer turnaround at this lot — large rigs should drop passengers at the trailhead and park back on the spur road.

**Mount Scott Trail** is the serious option — 4.4 miles, 1,300 feet of elevation gain, two and a half to three hours round trip at the park's highest trailhead, ending at the park's highest summit: 8,929 feet, with sweeping views of the entire Klamath Basin and the full lake below. It is the one place in the park where the entire caldera fits in a single camera frame. It is also the one trail where "the skip list says skip it on short trips" is genuinely good advice. Three hours of hard alpine hiking at high elevation, combined with three days of prior hiking, is a real physical ask. If it's day four of a four-day trip, honestly assess whether legs are actually fresh enough for this to be enjoyable, or whether a slow second pass of the West Rim Drive would serve you better. Mount Scott's reward is real and specific — but it earns it.

**For big-rig travelers specifically:** this is the zone built for you. The Pinnacles Road is the one paved spur in the park that terminates in a genuine large-vehicle turnaround. Everything else about the park rewards smaller vehicles and more nimble setups — this corner doesn't.

**The visitor divergence this zone resolves**

There are two types of Crater Lake visitors, and this zone serves one of them better than the rim does.

The first type came for the lake. They want to stand on the caldera edge and look down into the water. The rim is their park, and the Volcanic Outskirts is a detour they may or may not take depending on time. For them, this zone is a bonus — a pleasant half-day extension if the trip runs long enough to warrant it.

The second type came because they heard the park was beautiful and wants to spend time in the wilderness, but they don't need the lake specifically. They're the traveler who notices the crowds at rim overlooks and feels the pull to go somewhere quieter. For them, this zone might actually be the better version of the park: a forest waterfall, an untouched geological landscape, and a summit with a view that encompasses everything — including the lake from above, which is a perspective the rim itself can't give you.

If you're the second type, build a day around this zone rather than treating it as an add-on. Plaikni Falls in the morning, the Pinnacles in the early afternoon, and Mount Scott if the legs can handle it — that's a genuinely complete day in a different corner of the park, with none of the rim's parking anxiety and a fraction of the crowds.
